Color Theory: It's Simpler Than You Think

Let's imagine a scenario: You are repainting your bedroom a nice shade of green. You pick out a lovely mint color at the hardware store. You get your gallon of paint and paint rollers and head home. When you get home ready to paint, you notice that the mint color now looks like a grass green color. You're confused. You are certain you bought a lighter green. You double check the paint sample, the reciepts, it is the same shade of green. What happened? That is color theory at its finest.

Color theory is how colors behave and react to other colors and light. In this scenario, that mint green looked more like grass green because of the lighting in your home and the surrounding colors. Basically, color can look different against other colors or depending on the light hitting it.


Color theory is a great trick to know because it is super helpful when picking colors and mixing colors. As mentioned in Techniques, you can apply color theory for mixing the perfect and exact color. Think of a color wheel, you got your primary colors: red, yellow and blue and your secondary colors: orange, green and purple. These sets of colors are opposite each other on the color wheel, meaning they are complimentary to each other. Also known as complimentary colors. There are also warm and cool colors. Warm colors are yellow, orange and red. Cool colors are blue, green and purple. Think of a sunset to remember warm colors.

Mixing Paints

If you are looking for a darker shade of red, add a little green. Need a darker shade of yellow? Add some purple. It is highly recommened to use the color wheel when mixing colors instead of mixing white and black to get darker or ligher colors. Using color theory when mixing paints will give you vibrant colors instead of desaturated and dull colors if you mixed them white or black.

If you like to paint portraits and people like I do, it might be tricky mixing that exact skin tone color. You can use color theory to help you! Mix white, yellow, red, and blue (not all at once) to get skin tone colors. To test this, take a small dot of white paint on your hand, then yellow, then red, then blue. Mix those on your hand until it matches your skin tone. Keep adding one of those colors each slowly until you got it. Using color theory, you are guarenteed to get the right color every time!
